## Decision 1 — OQ1: which wedge leads? Three live theories

The audit lists three ICP candidates (§5): SDK/library vendors · polyglot
platform teams · audit-critical environments. Three interpretations of how to
lead are on the table — treat them as hypotheses to pressure-test, not ranked:

- **Theory A — audit's documented lead: SDK/library vendors first.** Project
  mode emits native npm/cargo/pip/go-mod packages with transpiled native tests,
  so "the same library behaves identically everywhere" *is* literally these
  vendors' product promise. Cleanest, most demoable proof of the equivalence
  moat; matches current scope (self-contained logic + emitted packages, no FFI).

- **Theory B — orchestrator's read: lead with the guarantee, sequence the ICP
  as a funnel.** The wedge is the *guarantee itself* (provable cross-target
  equivalence + auditable/reproducible AI pipeline); the three ICPs are not
  mutually exclusive, they're a funnel ordered by shortest proof-to-value loop.
  Mostly agrees with A on the *opening* demo (SDK vendors = tightest equivalence
  story) but treats segment choice as go-to-market sequencing, not identity, and
  keeps the governance segment as the expansion path. (Honest caveat: a
  refinement of the audit, not an independent thesis.)

- **Theory C — operator's read: lead with the industry-wide governance/assurance
  shift, un-narrowed.** The market is moving *en masse* toward agentic
  development, and the next frontier is hard, audited boundaries — provable
  assurances around AI-built software. So the lead wedge is the assurance angle
  broadly (nearest to the audit's "audit-critical environments") but
  **explicitly not** scoped down to a compliance vertical: it is the whole
  market arriving at the problem Bock has spent two years building for, with 1.0
  landing exactly as demand for provable assurance in agentic codegen
  crystallizes. Supported by audit §2(b), §2(c), §10. Tension to resolve in
  session: A is the *sharpest demo*; C is the *largest market narrative* — are
  they one go-to-market or a near-term-wedge-vs-positioning split?
